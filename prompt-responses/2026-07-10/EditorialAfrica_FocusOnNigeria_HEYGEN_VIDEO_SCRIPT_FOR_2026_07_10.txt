ADEREMI:
Welcome to EditorialAfrica. I am Aderemi Olapoju.

ONYEKA:
And I am Onyeka Okeke. Today is Friday, July 10, 2026. This is Focus on Nigeria, where we strip away the political noise and bring you the unvarnished truth by dissecting the heaviest opinions from the nation’s leading editorial boards.

[VISUAL: Studio setting with 'Focus on Nigeria' watermark and Nigerian Flag overlaid top-right. Over-the-shoulder graphic on Aderemi's side showing a bold headline: "THE PFIPC SCANDAL: GBAJAGATE".]

ADEREMI:
Today, the Nigerian press is absolutely on fire. We are looking at a government engulfed in a bizarre accountability scandal, an opposition literally tearing itself apart in the courts, and a national budget that continues to look more like a slush fund than a blueprint for national development. We begin with the headline that is dominating the front page of Punch Newspapers this morning: The Presidential Foreign Intervention Promotion Council... or the PFIPC.

ONYEKA:
The editorial board is demanding one thing: an independent, forensic probe. Aderemi, what started as a whisper about a fake agency has snowballed into what the press is now universally calling "Gbajagate." Punch is asking the questions that the Presidency has utterly failed to answer. The government insists that the PFIPC is a phantom agency—that it simply does not exist. Yet, the editorial reveals a paper trail that is impossible to ignore. How did a non-existent agency receive a federal waiver in August 2025 to recruit 300 staff members? Worse still, how did this "fake" council suddenly appear under the Presidency with a massive allocation of 1.3 billion Naira in the 2026 Appropriation Bill?

[VISUAL: Studio setting with 'Focus on Nigeria' watermark and Nigerian Flag overlaid top-right. Camera switches to Aderemi solo. Graphic changes to an image of a gavel and scales of justice with the text: "DEMAND FOR FORENSIC PROBE".]

ADEREMI:
It defies all logic and bureaucratic protocol. The editorial goes further to expose that public officials allegedly demanded a 48 percent cut of the PFIPC’s proposed 22.4 billion Naira take-off grant. Punch points out that this agency supposedly operated out of the Federal Secretariat in Abuja. How does a ghost agency occupy physical public offices and deal with senior government functionaries without detection?

ONYEKA:
This is exactly why the editorial board argues that the Presidency’s premature dismissal of these allegations—effectively clearing senior officials before any official investigation even began—has completely eroded public confidence. Punch is calling for a panel of retired jurists, digital forensic experts, and anti-corruption specialists to follow the money. As the paper rightly notes, only extreme transparency can cure this level of institutional rot.

[VISUAL: Studio setting with 'Focus on Nigeria' watermark and Nigerian Flag overlaid top-right. Two-shot of Aderemi and Onyeka. A graphic appears between them showing school children alongside stacks of Naira notes, titled: "BUDGET PADDING?".]

ADEREMI:
And speaking of institutional rot, let’s turn our attention to the legislature. Daily Trust has delivered a stinging editorial today on the absolute bastardization of the 2026 budget. Specifically, they are looking at the National Commission for Almajiri and Out-of-School Children’s Education.

ONYEKA:
This is truly heartbreaking. Nigeria has one of the highest numbers of out-of-school children in the entire world. The Almajiri Commission was set up to tackle this specific, monumental crisis in the North. But what did Daily Trust uncover? The National Assembly has completely hijacked the commission's 2026 budget, stuffing it with constituency projects that have absolutely nothing to do with education or the Almajiri system.

ADEREMI:
It is a classic, tragic case of budget padding. The lawmakers are using an agency meant for the most vulnerable children in our society as a conduit to execute their political pet projects back home. The Daily Trust editorial is calling for an immediate end to this budgetary indiscipline. If the funds appropriated for education are not utilized for education, the demographic time-bomb we are sitting on will simply detonate.

[VISUAL: Studio setting with 'Focus on Nigeria' watermark and Nigerian Flag overlaid top-right. Camera cuts to Onyeka solo. A graphic depicting the logos of the PDP and Labour Party cracking down the middle, titled "OPPOSITION IN CRISIS".]

ONYEKA:
But while the ruling APC government grapples with scandals and budget manipulation, what is the opposition doing to hold them accountable? According to ThisDay and Leadership newspapers today, they are too busy fighting themselves. Today, July 10, all eyes are on Justice Salim Ibrahim at the Federal High Court. ThisDay’s editorial highlights the existential crisis inside the Peoples Democratic Party. We have the Interim National Working Committee locked in a bitter legal deathmatch with the faction loyal to FCT Minister Nyesom Wike.

ADEREMI:
The Independent National Electoral Commission, INEC, is caught right in the middle, failing to update its portal for the 2027 elections until the courts decide who actually owns the party. Leadership newspaper echoes this exact tragedy regarding the Labour Party. Their editorial laments that the massive political capital the LP built during the 2023 elections is being squandered on internal power struggles. The media is sending a very clear warning today: a fragmented, legally paralyzed opposition is essentially rolling out the red carpet for the ruling party in 2027.

[VISUAL: Studio setting with 'Focus on Nigeria' watermark and Nigerian Flag overlaid top-right. Camera cuts to Aderemi solo. Graphic shows a silhouette of Peter Obi and President Tinubu, titled: "POLICY OVER POLITICS".]

ADEREMI:
And this ties perfectly into the editorial from The Sun. They published a piece titled "Resignation is not leadership." The board took a critical look at the recent demands by opposition figure Peter Obi for President Tinubu to resign over the state of the economy. The Sun argues that demanding resignation is cheap sensationalism. The macroeconomic distortions, the foreign exchange imbalances, and the security crises did not just magically appear in May 2023. They are the result of decades of structural decay. The paper is challenging the opposition to present mathematically sound, alternative roadmaps rather than just playing to the gallery.

[VISUAL: Studio setting with 'Focus on Nigeria' watermark and Nigerian Flag overlaid top-right. Camera switches to Onyeka solo. A graphic showing the flags of Nigeria and South Africa with a cracked line between them.]

ONYEKA:
Before we close today's deep dive, we must look at our foreign policy. The Guardian has delivered a furious editorial condemning the renewed xenophobic attacks and extra-judicial killings of Nigerians in South Africa. The House of Representatives has asked the Federal Government to summon the South African High Commissioner, but The Guardian says that is simply not enough.

ADEREMI:
The editorial points out the bitter irony that Nigerians are being slaughtered in a country whose freedom Nigeria spent massive diplomatic and financial resources to secure during the apartheid era. The paper is demanding a stringent review of all bilateral and economic ties until the South African government can guarantee the safety of our citizens. Diplomatic slaps on the wrist are no longer acceptable.

[VISUAL: Studio setting with 'Focus on Nigeria' watermark and Nigerian Flag overlaid top-right. Wide shot of the studio desk. Both presenters looking solemnly at the camera.]

ONYEKA:
From the corridors of the Federal Secretariat in Abuja where phantom agencies supposedly operate, to the streets of South Africa, the Nigerian press is demanding accountability, transparency, and a profound respect for human life and public resources.

ADEREMI:
The clock is ticking toward 2027. The government must clear the air on the Gbajagate scandal, and the opposition must get its house in order. That is the ultimate consensus of the Nigerian media today.

ONYEKA:
Thank you for joining us on this Friday deep dive. I am Onyeka Okeke.

And I am Aderemi Olapoju. This has been EditorialAfrica - Focus on Nigeria. Have a reflective weekend, and we will see you on Monday.
