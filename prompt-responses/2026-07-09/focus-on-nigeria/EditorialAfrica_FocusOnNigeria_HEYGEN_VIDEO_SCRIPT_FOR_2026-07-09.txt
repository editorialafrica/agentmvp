We begin this morning with a question of absolute integrity. The ghost agency scandal—now universally known as Gbajagate—has dominated the headlines all week. But today, the media has shifted its investigative glare from the silent Presidency to the silent watchdog.

If you pick up Punch Newspapers this morning, the editorial board is expressing pure, unfiltered outrage. The editorial is titled, "Gbajagate: The EFCC's Deafening Silence is an Indictment." For days, the nation has been bombarded with leaked documents showing a non-existent agency, the Presidential Foreign Intervention Promotion Council, slated to receive a massive 1.3 billion Naira allocation in the 2026 supplementary budget.

Punch is asking a very simple, yet devastating question: Where is the Economic and Financial Crimes Commission? Why hasn’t the anti-graft agency initiated an independent, suo motu investigation into how a phantom bureaucracy recruits staff and secures federal funding? The editorial argues that the EFCC’s reluctance to probe the Presidency directly destroys its operational credibility. The media is clearly warning that if the EFCC only barks at political opponents and internet fraudsters, but suddenly loses its voice when billions are being moved within the corridors of executive power, then the entire anti-corruption war is nothing but a facade.

And this culture of impunity is not restricted to Abuja. It trickles all the way down to the state capitals. The Nation newspaper delivers a brutal editorial today on the looming labor crisis, titled "Labour's Ultimatum and the Governors' Fiscal Rascality."

The Nigerian Labour Congress has issued a final 72-hour strike ultimatum over the non-payment of the new national minimum wage. The Nation exposes the absolute hypocrisy of the state governors who continually claim their treasuries are empty. The editorial meticulously outlines how these same governors are simultaneously rejecting wage increases for starving civil servants, while comfortably approving billions of Naira for luxury armored vehicles and bloated cabinet logistics.

The paper warns that this impending strike will completely paralyze an already fragile economy. They are demanding an extreme, but highly popular solution: The federal government must unconditionally withhold the Federation Account allocations—the FAAC funds—of any state that fails to implement the wage structure by Monday. The working class can no longer be expected to subsidize the extravagant lifestyles of sub-national executives.

And speaking of sub-national executives, Vanguard reveals exactly how these governors are maintaining their grip on power and money at the grassroots level. Their editorial exposes what can only be described as a silent, legislative coup.

Following the Supreme Court's landmark ruling that granted financial autonomy to Local Governments, state governors panicked. Now, according to Vanguard, they are using state houses of assembly to rubber-stamp parallel "administrative maintenance" bills. These laws are unconstitutionally designed to funnel federal grassroots allocations right back into state-controlled joint accounts. Vanguard is calling on the federal judiciary to urgently strike down these parallel laws. State assemblies are acting as accomplices to gubernatorial theft, and the poorest citizens in the rural areas are paying the price.

And what is the price of this systemic failure? We are seeing it in our infrastructure and our food supply.

The Guardian takes on the power sector today following the sixth total collapse of the national power grid in 2026 alone. The editorial labels it a "Monumental National Disgrace." The board criticizes the Transmission Company of Nigeria for relying on obsolete grid infrastructure while billions of Naira disappear into phantom maintenance contracts. The Guardian demands the complete privatization and decentralization of the transmission network. We simply cannot build a modern, 21st-century economy on a fragile grid that plunges the entire nation into darkness on a bi-monthly basis.

Simultaneously, Daily Trust focuses on a different kind of darkness falling over the North-West. Their editorial, "As Zamfara Burns, Abuja Plays Politics," mourns the total loss of the 2026 planting season to banditry. The paper condemns the federal government for treating this agrarian crisis as a public relations issue. Daily Trust insists that heavily armed, localized agro-rangers must be deployed immediately. If we do not reclaim our farmlands from terrorists today, we are guaranteeing an apocalyptic nationwide famine by December.

Tomorrow’s ruling will either be the catalyst for a miraculous opposition rebirth, or it will be the final nail in the coffin.
